# Checkpoint 3: Game Test Summary

I created a web version of my game, available at [https://physball.app/](https://physball.app/). Because of this, I was able to make my questions for game-testers be entirely online, which I hope increased the number of people able to play and give feedback.

## Game Tester Questions

The form I gave for game-testers to fill out is [here](https://cryptpad.fr/form/#/2/form/view/loBZz9EW0ottzWhAHrYj9fCCr++HXmRHEH20gt2-tJc/). But for quick reading, here are the questions I asked:

* Would you describe the game as fun?
    * Options: "Yes, absolutely!", "Yes, kind of", "Somewhat", "Not really", and "Absoltely not!".
    * This question was to attempt to gauge the amount of Type-1 fun players had.
* Did you feel a sense of accomplishment after playing? (same answer options as previous)
    * This question was to attempt to gauge the amount of Type-2 fun players had.
* Do you feel like your experience in the game made a good story? (same answer options as previous)
    * This question was to attempt to gauge the amount of Type-3 fun players had.
* Would you share the game with friends? (same answer options as previous)
    * This question was to attempt to gauge the amount of Type-4 fun players had.
* Would you describe the game as visually appealing? (same answer options as previous)
    * This was just to see how well I did on the aesthetic side of the game.
* Were the audio cues helpful? (same answer options as previous)
    * This was to see if the audio portions of the game worked well.
* Did the level music improve the experience? (same answer options as previous)
    * This was to see if the music portions of the game worked well.
* Were you able to complete Level 1 at least once?
    * Options: "Yes, I completed it quickly and easily", "Yes, I completed it but it took some effort", "I tried to complete it but was not able to", "I did not get very far", and "Screw this".
    * This was to attempt to gauge the difficulty level of the game as well as the level I created, as well as people's enjoyment of the level.
* Did you try any of the tutorial levels? Select which tutorial levels you trie, if any.
    * Checkboxes: "Tutorial 1" and "Tutorial 2".
    * This was to attempt to gauge how much people engaged with the rest of the game.
* How complicated would you consider Level 1?
    * Options: "Too complicated", "Too simple", and "Just right".
    * This question was to gauge how players felt about the first level.
* Did you have trouble seeing the buttons that control the doors in Level 1?
    * Options: "I found them immediately", "It took me a little bit to find them", and "there were buttons controlling the doors?".
    * This question was my attempt to gauge how obvious the level's puzzle was to its players.
* If you were able to find the buttons, how obvious was it shich door the button controlled? (Optional)
    * Options: "It was quite obvious", "I would not have guessed if not for the sounds", and "I did not realize the buttons controlled the doors".
    * This question was my attempt to gauge how obvious the level's puzzle's requirements were to its players.
* Did you try to complete Level 1 throught any alternate methos? (without opening the door, etc.)
    * Options: "I did not try alternate methods", "I tried alternate methods but gave up", and "I completed the level through alternate methods".
    * This question was my attempt to gauge player's interest in understanding the game's mechanics in order to perform more challenging versions of the level.
* If you tried to complete the Level 1 through an alternate method, what was your alternate method? (Optional)
    * Text box.
    * This was because I wanted to see how creative players' alternate solutions to the level were.
* Do you have any thoughts on the game? (Optional)
    * Text box.
    * This was to just get players' general feedback on the game.
* If you encountered any bugs, please describe them. (Optional)
    * Text box.
    * This was to try and get feedback on any bugs I missed.
* If you were to design a level, how would you design it? (Optional)
    * Text box.
    * This was to try and gauge players' engagement with the game as well as creative interest in the game. If I find a game I really like, creating new levels is one of the things that I really like to think about.
* Do you have a background in gaming?
    * Options: "Yes, I play a lot of video games", "I play video games sometimes", "I rarely play video games", and "This is the first video game I have every playerd".
    * This was to try and gauge players' comfort with video games in general, to see how that affects how they feel about the rest of the game.
* How would you describe yourself? (Optional)
    * Text box.
    * This was also to try and gauge players' comfort with video games as well as just their general relationship to video games.
* Name (This is just so you can identify yourself if you choose.) (Optional)
    * Text field.
    * This was so I could tell if any of the people submitting answers were my friends, as they would likely have a skewed view of my games.

## Feedback

Through the online form, I obtained feedback from 8 different people, 3 of which identified themselves as my friends.

## Findings Summary

Most people described the game as exhibiting type-1 and type-2 fun. The highest-rated portion of the game was the type-2 fun. Most people did not describe the game as type-3 fun. However, all people playing the game said that they would share it with friends. Most people described the game as visually appealing, while one person said that there was too much bloom. Surprisingly, nobody described the audio cues as the most helpful option, and one person said the audio cues were not helpful at all. This may have been because the game's audio is sometimes glitchy on web and may not have worked at all for that one person. However, everyone said that the level music improved the experience to verying degrees.

Most players were able to complete level 1 at least once, most saying they completed it quickly, but one struggled while completing it, and two were not able to complete it at all.

All players also tried the two tutorial levels.

One player felt Level 1 was too complicated, one felt it was too simple, and the rest felt it was just right.

Only one player had trouble seeing the buttons that control the doors. This was likely because there was a period of about a day when I first published the game where I had not made the buttons glow. After receiving this feedback, I decided to make the buttons glow.

All players were eventually able to find the buttons and all players said it was quite obvious which door each button corresponded to.

Six out of the eight players who gave feedback attempted to complete the level through alternate methods, but only one succeeded. The one who succeeded was one of my friends who I watched spend several hours perfecting a high-speed collision off a railing, allowing her to sail over the door where there was no kill-plane. Most other players attempted to bounce off the button to fly next to the door, but I had decided to put a kill plane there. Some people also tried launching themselves across the larger gap to the finish, but were unable to reach it. Some other people tried other methods of launching themselves over the door but were unable to land properly.

As for thoughts on the game, many people said they liked the controls and the physics aspects of the game, though some people felt they were slightly sluggish and hard to control. Some people felt that the second ball was especially hard to control. One person felt that the triggered change in music was uncalled for. One player asked for arrow-key-based navigation and controls.

For bugs, the biggest bug was the fact that, currently, the camera stays a fixed distance behind the ball, meaning that it can clip through walls, making it hard to see the ball. Many people also encountered audio issues on the web version of the game. One person encounterd screen tearing on the desktop version of the game. Many people also pointed out that it was quite easy to soft-lock the level and need to restart, like if the 2nd ball rolls through one of the doors and falls off. Some people also encountered some bugs in the physics engine where the 2nd ball would get stuck in place behind the first door, preveinting it from rolling down.

For designing a level, 5 of the 8 players provided ideas for new levels. Many of the players were interested in more difficult levels involving precision movement while one was interested in an easier level where you would move a cube instead of rolling a 2nd ball. One player in particular provided several intriguing level ideas, as well as describing some existing mechanics within my game that I did not even consider.

Finally, the majority of players said they play a lot of video games, usually describing themselves either as gamers or game-designers. There was one person who rarely played games but who worked at a game company.

## Comparison

I do not have any team mates working on my game with me, so I will compare the feedback with what I expected the feedback to be.

First off, I expected the highest type of fun to be type-2 fun, but I expected type-3 fun to also be higher, as my mother seemed quite frustrated while attempting the level but laughed about it a lot before giving up. I was surprised by the fact that nobody described the audio cues as especially helpful, as I had thought they added significantly to the experience. I was also surprised by the fact that everyone said that the level music improved the experience, as I expected the glitchy-ness of the web-version's audio to be a dealbreaker for some in that regard.

I expected more players to struggle to complete level 1 but still actually complete it.

I also expected a lot more players to say that the first level was either too simple or too complicated.

As for trouble seeing the buttons, I expected them to be hard to see before I made them glow. However, I did not expect everyone else to find them so easily once I made them glow.

I also exptected come confusion as to which buttons controlled which doors, and was somewhat surprised that nobody had any.

The number of players attempting to complete the level without opening the door was about what I expected. However, I expected several more players to succeed at completing the level without opening the door.

With the thoughts on the game, I expected more players to have trouble with the controls being acceleration-based instead of movement-based, meaning that pressing the forward button speeds you up in the forward direction, and then pressing the backward button first only slows you down in the forward direction. However many players seemed to find it satisfying. The problems with controlling the 2nd ball were pretty much entirely expected though.

People found a significant amount of bugs which I never encountered, especially things like the screen tearing on desktop. Most of the bugs were in parts of the game that I do not directly have control over, and thus do not know if I can fix.

I expected many players to have simple level ideas which would be difficult to implement given the state of the engine, but instead most players suggested interesting levels or level ideas that would be easily accomplished with what I have of the engnine so far.

I also did not expect nearly as many of the players to be so comfortable with playing video games, as I only sometimes play video games myself. I expected the majority of the players to be like me. Instead, many of the players seemed to easily get better at my own game than I was.
